Evolving the Content

How We're Pushing the Format Forward

The best brands on Instagram don't copy what works in their niche — they create their own formats. Here's exactly how we're evolving the Up and Running content approach in the next phase.

Spring race season is one of the highest-engagement periods in the running community — 5Ks, half marathons, marathons, and training cycles are all live right now. Up and Running is tightly connected to this world. We're not just going to cover it — we're going to own it visually with tools and formats that no other local running store is using. The goal isn't to go viral. It's to create content so specific to Up and Running and the Dayton running community that it's immediately recognizable as yours.

New Tool — Drone

Aerial Race and Event Coverage

Drone footage at spring races and community events creates a visual perspective no one else in your market has. It signals investment, production quality, and community presence simultaneously — and it stops the scroll.

  • Overhead start-line shot at a local 5K or half marathon featuring UAR-sponsored runners
  • Aerial coverage of the Frozen Feet Challenge 2.0 — showing the scale of the community event
  • Race day recap Reel: drone establishing shot → ground-level fitting content → finish line celebration
  • Spring training montage — drone follows a runner through a Dayton trail or park route
New Tool — Meta Glasses

First-Person POV Content

Meta glasses create an authentic, unfiltered POV that feels completely different from produced Reels. It's the format that makes the audience feel like they're inside the store, inside the run, inside the community — not watching from the outside.

  • POV: Expert fitting session from the fitter's perspective — "here's what I see when I watch you walk"
  • POV: Run club Tuesday night — first-person view of the group heading out from the store
  • POV: Race morning gear prep — staff member walking the course, checking in with runners
  • POV: Behind the counter — a day in the life of the Up and Running team
Spring Season — Race Content

Owning the Spring Race Narrative

Spring race season is a massive organic content opportunity for a specialty running store. Every race in the Dayton-Cincinnati region is a chance to show up, document, and position Up and Running as the community's home base before, during, and after race day.

  • "Race Week Checklist" carousel — gear, nutrition, and shoe tips from the UAR team
  • Pre-race fitting Reel: "Running your first 5K? Here's what to wear on race day"
  • Race day coverage: start line, community moments, finish line celebrations
  • Post-race recap: community photos, results shoutouts, "see you at the next one" CTA
Format Strategy

Building UAR-Specific Recurring Formats

The highest-performing accounts on Instagram don't just post good content — they create formats the audience comes back for. We're building 2–3 recurring series that are unmistakably Up and Running.

  • "Fitting Friday" — weekly short-form Reel showing one specific fitting insight or shoe recommendation
  • "Run Club Recap" — weekly Tuesday night run group coverage, building community habit and FOMO
  • "Race Season Spotlight" — monthly feature on a UAR community member's race goal and their gear setup
  • "Ask the Fitter" — Q&A format addressing the most common shoe and fitting questions from the community

The principle: In today's creator market, copying what works in your niche is a race to average. The brands that build real audiences create their own visual language — formats, perspectives, and series that are so specific to them that no one else can replicate it. That's what we're building for Up and Running. Not content that looks like every other running store. Content that only Up and Running could make.

Important Context

The industry-standard model uses conservative assumptions. The 50,700 unique reach figure is the actual number of unique accounts Instagram served your content to — not total views (which count the same person multiple times). The 1% lead rate and 20% conversion rate are widely cited benchmarks used across social media ROI analysis. These numbers are the floor, not the ceiling — they don't account for in-store walk-ins driven by social, word-of-mouth referrals from content, or the long-term brand equity built by 5 months of consistent, community-first content.
